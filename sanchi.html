Sanchi Stupa

A Monument of Buddhist Heritage

Introduction

The Sanchi Stupa, located in the state of Madhya Pradesh, is one of the most famous and important Buddhist monuments in India. Built in the 3rd century BCE by Emperor Ashoka, it houses relics of the Buddha and is a symbol of peace and harmony.

Historical Significance

Sanchi Stupa stands as one of the oldest stone structures in India. It was commissioned by Emperor Ashoka after his conversion to Buddhism. The stupa, which means "heap," represents the burial mound of the Buddha and serves as a pilgrimage site for followers of Buddhism.

Architectural Highlights

Sanchi is a religious and archaeological site in Madhya Pradesh, India that is home to a group of Buddhist monuments, including stupas, pillars, palaces, temples, and monasteries:Sanchi is the oldest Buddhist sanctuary in the world and was a major center of Buddhism from the 3rd century BC to the 12th century AD. The site's history includes:
Construction: The Great Stupa, or Stupa No. 1, was built during the Mauryan Empire and decorated with one of Ashoka's Pillars. Smaller stupas, such as Stupa No. 2 and Stupa No. 3, were built later. Discovery: General Taylor discovered the site in 1818 after it had been abandoned for 600 years.
Restoration: Major restoration work was carried out in 1912 by archaeologist John Marshall and Buddhist scholar Albert Foucher.
On a hill overlooking the plain and about 40 km from Bhopal, the site of Sanchi comprises a group of Buddhist monuments (monolithic pillars, palaces, temples and monasteries) all in different states of conservation most of which date back to the 2nd and 1st centuries B.C. It is the oldest Buddhist sanctuary in existence and was a major Buddhist centre in India until the 12th century A.D. Outstanding Universal Value
The stupas, temples, viharas, and stambha at Sanchi in central India are among the oldest and most mature examples of aniconic arts and free-standing architecture that comprehensively document the history of Buddhism from the 3rd century BCE to the 12th century CE. About 10 km from Vidisha, the Buddhist monuments at Sanchi, located on a serene and picturesque forested plateau, are also considered to be the sacrosanct Cetiyagiri in the Sri Lankan Buddhist chronicles, where Mahindra, the son of Emperor Aśoka, stopped prior to undertaking his journey as a missionary to Sri Lanka. The enshrined remains of Sariputra and Maudgalyayana (chief disciples of Buddha) in Sanchi were venerated by Theravadins, and continue to be revered to the present day.
The inception of Sanchi as a sacred centre is attributed to the Mauryan emperor Aśoka. His reign in the 3rd century BCE is considered instrumental to the spread of Buddhism throughout the Indian subcontinent. With the establishment of the monolithic Aśoka Stambha (pillar) bearing a highly elaborate capital, Emperor Aśoka distinguished Sanchi as a site of great importance. Contemporary with the stambha was a brick stupa, which was later increased in scale during the Sunga dynasty (184-72 BCE), covered with an ashlar stone veneer, and augmented with circumambulatory paths and staircases with ornate balustrades, harmika, yashti, chhatra, and four torana, which were later ornamented during the Satavahanas dynasty in the 1st century CE.
The last addition to the grand stupa was during the Gupta dynasty (5th century CE), when four shrines were added at the cardinal entry points. Today, this grand structure of Sanchi (“Stupa 1”) is considered an incomparable example of the mature phase of Indian stupas. Since Aśokan times, subsequent powerful empires that reigned over this region - such as the Sunga, Kushana, Kshatrapa, and finally Gupta dynasties - continued to contribute to the expansion of Sanchi with the construction of hypostyle, apsidal, and other temples and shrines, comparatively smaller stupas (Stupas 2 and 3), and numerous viharas. Corroborated by inscriptions present in the property, Sanchi remained an important seat of Buddhism until the 13th century CE.
The Buddhist monuments at Sanchi contain an appreciable concentration of early Indian artistic techniques and Buddhist art, referred to as its Anionic School or Phase. Depicting Buddha through symbols, the sculpted art shows the evolution in sculpting techniques and the elaboration of icons, especially depicting Buddha. Stories and facts of great religious and historical significance, enlivened with bas-relief and high-relief techniques, are also depicted. The quality of craftsmanship in representing the gamut of symbolism through plants, animals, human beings, and Jataka stories shows the development of art though the integration of indigenous and non-indigenous sculpting traditions.
Criterion (i): The perfection of its proportions and the richness of the sculpted decorative work on its four gateways make Stupa 1 an incomparable artistic achievement. The group of Buddhist monuments at Sanchi – stupas, temples and monasteries – is unique in India because of its age and quality.
Criterion (ii): From the time that the oldest preserved monument on the site was erected, i.e., Aśoka’s column with its projecting capital of lions inspired by Achaemenid art, Sanchi’s role as intermediary for the spread of cultures and their peripheral arts throughout the Mauryan Empire, and later in India of the Sunga, Shatavahana, Kushan and Gupta dynasties, was confirmed.
Criterion (iii): Having remained a principal centre of Buddhism up to early medieval India following the spread of Hinduism, Sanchi bears unique witness as a major Buddhist sanctuary in the period from the 3rd century BCE to the 1st century CE.
Criterion (iv): The stupas at Sanchi, in particular Stupa 1 and Stupa 3, represent the most accomplished form of this type of monument. The hemispherical, egg-shaped dome (anda), topped with a cubical relic chamber (harmika), is built on a circular terrace (medhi); it has one or two ambulatories for the faithful to use (pradakshina patha). Representing a transition from wood structures to stone, the railings (vedika) and the gateways (torana) also bear witness to the continued use of the primitive forms of megalithic tumuli covered with an outer layer and surrounded by a palisade.
Criterion (v): Sanchi is one of the oldest extant Buddhist sanctuaries. Although Buddha never visited the site during any of his former lives or during his earthly existence, the religious nature of this shrine is obvious. The chamber of relics of Stupa 3 contained the remains of Sariputra, a disciple of Shakyamuni who died six months before his master; he is especially venerated by the occupants of the “small vehicle” or Hinayana.
Integrity
Within the boundaries of the property are all the known elements necessary to express its Outstanding Universal Value, including the sculpted monolithic pillars, sanctuaries, temples, and viharas atop and along the slopes of the hillock of Sanchi. These elements demonstrate the complete vocabulary of mature Buddhist aniconic art and free-standing architecture. The property, which also encompasses its near natural setting, is thus of adequate size to ensure the complete representation of the features and processes that convey the significance of the Buddhist Monuments at Sanchi. The property is in a good state of conservation. Threats and potential threats to the integrity of the property include pressure from the local villagers to use the right-of-way in the prohibited area (as was the case in the historic past), incursions into this area, and development in the villages.
Authenticity
The archaeological remains of the Buddhist Monuments at Sanchi are authentic in terms of their locations and setting, forms and design, and materials and substance, as well as, to a degree, their spirit. These representations of mature Buddhist free-standing architecture and aniconic sculpted art remain at their original locations and in a setting that is sympathetic. The Sanchi stupas (numbered 1, 2, and 3) were restored in the early 20th century and demonstrate all the original features characteristic of mature Indian stupas. Though abandoned for about 600 years, Sanchi has witnessed the revival of a pilgrimage from all over the Buddhist world, and in particular from Sri Lanka, thus testifying to the religious significance of this place. The site is alive with chants and prayers to immortalize the remains of Sariputra and Maudgalyayana, two of the foremost disciples of Lord Buddha.

Religious Importance

Sanchi is a significant pilgrimage site for Buddhists, symbolizing the spread of Buddhism throughout India. It plays a crucial role in the preservation and promotion of Buddhist philosophy and teachings.

1. History of Sanchi

The history of Sanchi dates back to the 3rd century BCE, when the Great Stupa was commissioned by Emperor Ashoka, a significant figure in Indian history who embraced Buddhism after the Kalinga War. After adopting the teachings of Buddha, Ashoka built several stupas to spread the message of Buddhism. The Great Stupa at Sanchi was originally a simple hemispherical structure containing the relics of the Buddha. Commissioning by Ashoka: Emperor Ashoka, who ruled over a vast empire, erected the Great Stupa at Sanchi to honor the Buddha and mark the spread of Buddhism. Subsequent Expansions: In the 1st century BCE, during the reign of the Shunga dynasty, the stupa was expanded with more detailed ornamentation, including the addition of the stone railings and the four elaborately carved gateways (Toranas).

2. Architectural Significance

Sanchi’s architecture is a blend of simplicity, symbolism, and artistry. It reflects the development of Buddhist art and architecture over several centuries. The complex comprises stupas, temples, monasteries, and Ashokan pillars. The Great Stupa: The hemispherical mound of the stupa represents the burial mound of the Buddha and is a symbol of enlightenment. The structure itself stands on a raised terrace, with a harmika on top, representing the bodhi tree under which Buddha attained enlightenment. The chatra (umbrella-like structure) above symbolizes the protection of the Buddha's relics. The Four Gateways (Toranas): These four gates, facing the cardinal directions, are intricately carved with images from the Jataka Tales, which narrate stories from the Buddha's previous lives. The reliefs on the toranas are considered masterpieces of ancient Indian art, depicting events from the life of Buddha, like his birth, renunciation, enlightenment, and first sermon. Ashoka Pillar: One of the major features of the Sanchi complex is the Ashokan Pillar, which carries inscriptions and is adorned with carvings. This pillar once had the four-lion capital, which was later adapted as the national emblem of India. The Ashoka Pillar at Sanchi is also known for its polished stone surface and its beautiful lotus-shaped capital.

3. Religious Significance

Sanchi is one of the most significant religious centers for Buddhists. Though Buddha never visited the site personally, it became a major pilgrimage center after Ashoka’s adoption of Buddhism. The complex is a symbol of peace, non-violence, and the teachings of Buddha. Buddhist Pilgrimage Site: Sanchi has attracted Buddhist pilgrims from all over the world for centuries. It is one of the places where Buddhism flourished and spread to other parts of India and beyond. Spread of Buddhist Teachings: The stupa itself, along with the carvings and inscriptions, represents the propagation of the Buddha’s teachings on dharma, karma, and samsara (cycle of rebirth). The structures of Sanchi were designed to inspire meditation, introspection, and spiritual growth for the followers of Buddhism.

4. Restoration and Preservation

Over the centuries, Sanchi fell into disrepair after the decline of Buddhism in India. However, during the 19th century, British archaeologists rediscovered Sanchi and began restoration efforts. Alexander Cunningham’s Role: In the 19th century, British archaeologist Alexander Cunningham led the efforts to restore the Great Stupa and other monuments at Sanchi. Thanks to these efforts, the site was preserved, and its significance re-established. UNESCO World Heritage Site: In 1989, the Sanchi Stupa and its surrounding monuments were declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site, recognizing its outstanding universal value as an ancient Buddhist center of art, culture, and architecture.

5. Important Structures at Sanchi

While the Great Stupa is the central monument, the Sanchi complex includes various other stupas, temples, monasteries, and shrines, each with its unique history and features. Stupa No. 2 and Stupa No. 3: These smaller stupas are also significant. Stupa No. 3 houses relics of two of the Buddha’s chief disciples—Sariputra and Mahamoggallana. Temple 17: Dating to the 5th century CE, Temple 17 is an early example of Gupta architecture. It’s considered one of the finest examples of classical Indian architecture. Temple 45: This was the last significant structure added to the Sanchi complex in the 7th century CE.

6. Cultural and Artistic Legacy

he Sanchi Stupa and its associated monuments are celebrated for their artistic legacy, particularly the elaborate carvings on the gateways. The Jataka Tales depicted on the toranas provide a visual account of the Buddha’s life and teachings, making Sanchi not only a religious site but also an important source of Buddhist art and iconography. Narrative Art: The carvings are a form of narrative art, depicting not only religious themes but also aspects of everyday life during ancient times. The art at Sanchi influenced the subsequent development of Indian sculpture and architecture.

7. Visitor Experience

Tourist Attraction: Sanchi attracts both tourists and spiritual seekers from around the world. The peaceful environment and historical richness of the place offer a unique experience. Educational Visits: Scholars, historians, and students frequently visit Sanchi to study its history, architecture, and cultural significance.

8. Sanchi in Modern Times

Sanchi remains a prominent site for archaeological research and is a major tourist attraction in India. Efforts to preserve the site continue, ensuring that future generations can appreciate this gem of ancient India’s Buddhist heritage.

LOCATION OF SANCHI STUP


The Sanchi Stupa is located in the village of Sanchi in the Raisen district of Madhya Pradesh, India. It is situated approximately: 46 kilometers (28 miles) northeast of Bhopal, the capital city of Madhya Pradesh. The exact geographical coordinates of Sanchi are 23.4861° N latitude and 77.7416° E longitude. The stupa is built on a hilltop, about 91 meters (300 feet) above the surrounding plain, offering a commanding view of the surrounding countryside. Its strategic location near Bhopal makes it accessible for tourists and pilgrims. How to Reach Sanchi: By Air: The nearest airport is in Bhopal, located about 55 kilometers away. By Train: The nearest railway station is Sanchi Railway Station, which is connected to Bhopal and other nearby cities. By Road: Sanchi is well-connected by road, and regular buses and taxis operate from Bhopal and other nearby towns.
